<h2><strong><span style="color: #000000;">“It Was Just a Bit of Rain…” Until It Wasn’t</span></strong></h2>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">We’ve all brushed it off. That one flickering light in the hallway. The switch that sparks when touched. The power point that hums if you plug something heavy-duty into it. Alone, these things feel harmless and annoying, but not dangerous. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">The thing is, water and old wiring are a match made in electrical hell. A single thunderstorm, especially paired with aging or overloaded circuits, can trigger short circuits, blown fuses, and in the worst cases, electrical fires. And Melbourne’s erratic weather? It does not play favourites. From Eltham to Diamond Creek, homes built decades ago are still running on electrical wiring that was never meant to handle today’s tech-heavy lifestyle.</span></p>
<h2><strong><span style="color: #000000;">What a Wiring Check Actually Involves</span></strong></h2>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">You might be picturing someone pulling all your walls apart like a renovation gone rogue. Don’t worry, it’s rarely like that. A proper electrical wiring inspection, especially one done before storm season, is usually a mix of visual inspections of your switchboard and meter, testing outlets and circuits, checking for exposed wires, and verifying that safety switches actually work when they’re supposed to. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">If you’re living in an older home, or if your power trips every time the kettle and microwave are on at once, this check isn’t a luxury. It;s a necessity. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Lets paint a picture: a local family in Greensborough had a small power cut during a December storm last year. At first, no big deal, just a reset on the safety switch. But later that night, the smell or burning plastic woke them up. Turns out water had entered an external power point and was heating the wires from the inside. Luckily, they got out in time. The fire didn’t spread. But the electrician said if they’d waited one more hour, not so good things would’ve happened. </span></p>

<p><span style="color: #000000;">There’s this idea that an emergency electrician is only for blackouts and power outages. But it’s way broader than that. Flickering lights and overheating outlets are just some of the signs that something’s wrong. Some of the most common post-storm calls electricians get include water-damaged switchboards, burnt-out safety switches, surges frying TVs and fridges, and entire houses going dark of course. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">And it’s not just homes obviously. Businesses, especially cafes, clinics, and retail stores, can’t afford to be offline for even an hour. No EFTPOS, no lights, no customers, no business. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">So if your lights go out, sure, that’s an emergency. But if something just feels off with your power, call an electrician. It’s better to feel silly than to feel smoke. </span></p>
