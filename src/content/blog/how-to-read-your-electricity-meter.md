<p>Your electricity meter is the cash register of your home's energy use, and yet most people never look at it until a bill seems wrong. Reading it yourself takes a minute, catches estimated-billing errors, and is the first step in understanding where your energy goes. Here's how to read each common meter type — dials, digital displays and smart meters — plus what the numbers actually mean.</p>

<h2>First: what the meter measures</h2>
<p>Domestic electricity meters count <strong>kilowatt-hours (kWh)</strong> — units of energy, not power. One kWh is a thousand watts sustained for one hour: a 2,000&nbsp;W kettle running for 30 minutes, a 100&nbsp;W television running for ten hours. Your supplier multiplies the kWh you've used by your tariff rate; everything else on the bill is standing charges and taxes.</p>

<h2>Reading a digital meter</h2>
<p>Modern non-smart meters show a straightforward digital display:</p>
<ol>
<li>Read the digits from left to right.</li>
<li><strong>Ignore anything in red or after a decimal point</strong> — those are tenths of a unit.</li>
<li>If the display cycles through several values (rate 1, rate 2, total, time), note the register numbers your tariff uses. Two-rate tariffs (day/night) show two separate registers, often labelled R1 and R2 or Low/Normal.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Reading a dial meter</h2>
<p>Older electromechanical meters — like the classic spinning-disc unit pictured above — use a row of clock-style dials. They intimidate people, but three rules tame them:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Adjacent dials rotate in opposite directions.</strong> Check each dial's own 0–9 direction rather than assuming.</li>
<li><strong>When the pointer sits between two numbers, record the lower one</strong> (between 9 and 0, record 9).</li>
<li><strong>When a pointer sits exactly on a number</strong>, look at the dial to its right: if that dial reads 8 or 9, the pointer hasn't actually completed the digit — record one less.</li>
</ol>
<p>Read the dials left to right, skip any dial marked 1/10, and you have your reading. Take a phone photo as a timestamped record — useful if you ever dispute a bill.</p>

<h2>Reading a smart meter</h2>
<p>Smart meters send readings to your supplier automatically, but you can always read them locally:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>On the meter itself</strong>, the display either shows total kWh directly or reveals it after pressing a button (often labelled A or 9 depending on the model — the supplier's instructions or nameplate will say).</li>
<li><strong>On the in-home display</strong>, you get live consumption in watts plus daily/weekly/monthly kWh. The live figure is the fun one: walk around switching things on and off and watch what each appliance really draws.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Turning readings into insight</h2>
<p>One reading tells your supplier the truth; two readings tell <em>you</em> the truth. Note the meter reading at the same time each week and subtract: that's your weekly kWh. From there:</p>
<ul>
<li>Compare a heating week against a mild week to see what climate control really costs.</li>
<li>Check standby load: read the meter last thing at night and first thing in the morning. A large overnight number with everything "off" means fridges plus a pile of standby devices — or something you didn't know was running.</li>
<li>Sanity-check your bill's estimated readings; suppliers correct genuine errors when you submit actual readings.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What meter reading is <em>not</em></h2>
<p>Reading the display is completely safe. Everything else about a meter is not your territory: the meter, its terminals and the service fuse ahead of it belong to your supplier or network operator, seals and all. Signs of trouble at the meter — burning smells, buzzing, scorch marks, a display that's dead — mean calling your supplier, not opening anything. And connecting or moving a meter is strictly professional work, as our article on <a href="/how-to-connect-a-home-electricity-meter/">home electricity meter connection</a> explains in detail.</p>

<div class="callout" role="note"><p><strong>Note:</strong> display sequences, button labels and tariff register names vary by country and meter model. When in doubt, your electricity supplier's website will have instructions for the exact meters they install.</p></div>
